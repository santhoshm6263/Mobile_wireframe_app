User Research
Understanding who we design for — their daily challenges with mental health and what they need from a digital wellness companion.
Problem Statement
Young adults (18–35) in high-stress environments struggle with anxiety, burnout, and emotional overwhelm — yet find traditional therapy inaccessible due to cost, stigma, or time constraints. They need a low-barrier, private, and supportive digital tool to manage their mental wellbeing daily.
Target User Segments
Primary Users
College students and early-career professionals aged 18–30 dealing with academic or workplace stress, often first-time mental health app users.
Secondary Users
Working professionals aged 30–40 managing burnout, parenting, or work-life balance issues who have tried therapy and want a daily supplement.
Key User Needs
Track mood without effort — quick daily check-ins, not lengthy forms
Private, judgment-free journaling to process emotions
Quick, guided breathing or mindfulness for stressful moments
See their emotional patterns over time to build self-awareness
Pain Points Identified
Existing apps feel clinical or overwhelming
Users abandon apps with too many features, complex onboarding, or sterile medical UI.
Forgetting to log moods
Without gentle reminders, users miss daily check-ins and lose streak motivation.
No sense of progress
Users give up when they don't see meaningful insights or improvement over time.
Privacy concerns
Many users fear their mental health data could be shared or exposed.

What these personas tell us about the design
Both users need a fast, non-intimidating entry point. Priya needs encouragement and simplicity; Arjun needs depth and data. The app must serve both — a simple surface with optional depth. Onboarding should take under 2 minutes, and the daily check-in under 30 seconds.
User Flow
The path a new user takes from launching the app for the first time to completing their first mood check-in and journaling session — the core value action.
Key Task: Complete first mood check-in + journal entry
1
App launch — Splash Screen
Logo animates in. App checks auth status. New user → goes to Onboarding. Returning user → goes to Home.
2
Onboarding (3 slides)
Quick swipeable slides explaining core features. No login required yet. "Get Started" button on slide 3.
3
Sign Up / Login
Email + password or Google sign-in. Phone number optional. Privacy policy displayed clearly. Estimated time: 60 seconds.
4
Home Screen
Personalised greeting, daily check-in prompt card, streak tracker, and quick-access tiles for Journal, Breathe, and Insights.
5
Mood Check-in (tap "How are you feeling?")
5 emotion tiles (Happy, Calm, Neutral, Anxious, Sad). User taps one. Optional: add sub-emotions tags. Takes under 15 seconds.
6
Journal Prompt (optional, auto-suggested)
AI-generated prompt based on mood selected (e.g. "What made you feel anxious today?"). Free-form text entry with voice option.
7
Completion + Insight nudge
Positive affirmation shown. Quick insight: "You've felt calm 3 times this week." Option to explore Breathe or Insights.
8
Home (updated state)
Check-in card now shows today's logged mood. Streak counter incremented. User can navigate to other sections via bottom nav.
Decision points in the flow
At Step 6 (Journal), the user can skip — this is a soft optional step, never forced. At Step 7, the user can dismiss the insight nudge. These choices prevent the app from feeling like a chore and respect user autonomy — a core mental wellness design principle.

Design Thinking Process
Design Thinking is a 5-stage problem-solving framework used by top product teams. Think of it as the mindset behind every decision in this case study — not just a checklist, but a way of building empathy into the product.
👁
1. Empathize
We step into the user's shoes — understanding their emotions, environment, and context without judgment. For MindMate, we imagined a student at 2am, anxious before an exam, opening an app for the first time.
Output: User interviews insights, affinity maps, empathy maps for Priya and Arjun
🎯
2. Define
We translate observations into a clear problem statement. Not "build a meditation app" but: "People need a low-effort, private way to recognise and track their emotional state daily."
Output: Problem statement, How Might We questions, user pain point matrix
💡
3. Ideate
We brainstorm without filtering. Ideas included: voice journaling, mood emojis, anonymous community spaces, streak rewards, AI-generated reflection prompts, and biometric mood detection. We then prioritised by impact vs effort.
Output: Feature list, priority matrix, chosen MVP features
📱
4. Prototype
We created these low-fidelity wireframes — fast, cheap to make, easy to change. The goal is NOT to make it pretty; it's to test whether the layout and flow make sense to a real user in 2 minutes.
Output: 6 wireframe screens (this case study), clickable Figma prototype
✔
5. Test
We show the prototype to real users — ideally 5 people from the target persona groups. We observe without guiding: "Can you find the check-in?" We listen for confusion, hesitation, and delight. Insights from testing loop back to Empathize and Define — the process is never linear.
Output: Usability test script, 5-user observation notes, iteration list for v2 wireframes
Why beginners should care about this process
Design Thinking prevents the most common mistake in UX: designing what you think users want instead of what they actually need. Each stage produces an artefact that can be included in your portfolio to show hiring managers your thinking process — not just your Figma skills.

Case Study Structure (Problem → Solution → Outcome)
Problem
Young adults in India and globally face rising mental health challenges but lack accessible, stigma-free, easy-to-use daily tools. Therapy is expensive; existing apps feel clinical or overwhelming.
Solution
MindMate — a minimal, privacy-first mobile app offering a 15-second daily mood check-in, AI-prompted journaling, guided breathing, and weekly emotional insights. Designed for one-handed, sleepy-morning use.
Outcome (projected after usability testing)
Target: 70% of users complete daily check-in for 7 days straight. Avg. onboarding time under 90 seconds. 80% rate the app as "not overwhelming." Journal entry completion rate of 40%+ when mood is Anxious or Sad.

Figma Conversion Guide (beginner-friendly)
1. Create a new Figma file → set frame size to 390 × 844px (iPhone 14)
2. For each wireframe screen: use rectangles (R) for all blocks, text (T) for labels
3. Keep everything in grayscale — use Fill: #F5F5F5 for cards, #E0E0E0 for bars
4. Use Auto Layout for buttons and cards (Shift+A) for easy resizing
5. Add a 16px grid (View → Layout Grids) to keep spacing consistent
6. Link screens with Prototype tab → arrows from button to next frame for click-through demo
